## 1. Modern VMI Landscape: Autopilot vs Hybrid vs Recommendation

### The VMI Spectrum (2024-2025)

Modern VMI systems fall into three main categories:

#### **A. Full Autopilot VMI (True VMI)**
- **Definition:** Supplier has authority to create and ship orders without customer approval
- **How it works:** Supplier monitors inventory levels and automatically generates purchase orders that are firm commitments
- **When it's used:**
  - High-volume, low-value consumables (e.g., fasteners, office supplies)
  - Mature, highly trusted supplier relationships (3+ years)
  - Products where stockout risk is moderate
  - Retail/consumer goods with predictable demand patterns

**Example:** Automotive manufacturers use full autopilot VMI with suppliers of standard fasteners and electronic components. The parts supplier accesses real-time production data and automatically synchronizes shipments.

**Industry data:** According to 2023 supply chain studies, only **20-30% of VMI relationships** operate in full autopilot mode, and these typically took 2-3 years to reach that trust level.

#### **B. Hybrid/Co-Managed Inventory (CMI)**
- **Definition:** Supplier generates recommendations, but customer retains final approval authority
- **How it works:** Supplier has visibility to inventory data, creates forecasts and replenishment orders in customer's system, but orders aren't firm until customer approves
- **When it's used:**
  - High-value, high-risk products
  - New VMI relationships (first 1-2 years)
  - Products with long lead times (>30 days)
  - Industries where stockouts are catastrophic

**Critical distinction (from NetSuite research):**
> "The key difference between CMI and VMI is that in CMI, the order placed by the supplier is just a **recommendation** and is not a confirmed order unless the customer approves it. In VMI, the order generated by the supplier is a **confirmed order** to deliver and bill."

**Example:** Healthcare products manufacturer implemented hybrid VMI with their #1 customer, generating recommendations based on distribution center inventory. Result: 20% revenue increase, "Supplier of the Year" recognition, and gradual transition to fuller automation.

#### **C. Recommendation-Only (Vendor Advisory)**
- **Definition:** Supplier provides data-driven insights and suggestions, customer places all orders
- **How it works:** Supplier shares analysis, forecasts, and alerts, but has no ability to create orders
- **When it's used:**
  - Early-stage vendor relationships
  - Highly customized products
  - Customers who require full control

**Modern trend:** According to 2024 VMI software research, there's a shift toward **"human-in-the-loop" workflows** where AI provides recommendations but humans approve critical decisions.

### Long Lead Time Products (45+ days)

Research shows that products with **lead times over 30 days** almost universally use hybrid approval models initially, for these reasons:

1. **Error cost is extremely high** - A wrong order takes 45+ days to correct
2. **Forecast accuracy decreases** - Harder to predict demand 45+ days out
3. **Customer anxiety is higher** - Need reassurance and control during transition
4. **Safety stock must be higher** - Formula: For 45-day max lead time, safety stock can be 1-2 months of inventory

**Example calculation (from safety stock research):**
- Maximum lead time: 45 days
- Average lead time: 30 days
- Maximum daily usage: 128 units
- Average daily usage: 64 units
- **Safety stock = (128 Ã— 45) - (64 Ã— 30) = 3,840 units**

For MyPak's customers using 12,000 cartons/week, this means recommending safety stock of **8-10 weeks** of inventory for a 45-day lead time.

## 3. Trust & Transparency Patterns

### The Trust Problem in VMI

Multiple research studies identify **trust as the #1 barrier to VMI adoption**:

**Baltimore distributor case study (Inbound Logistics):**
> "How will the guy using a computer in Cincinnati do a better job managing my inventory than I can do right here in Baltimore? I just can't trust this idea."

**Key research findings on trust barriers:**

1. **Control anxiety** - "Relinquishing control of inventory seems like a high-risk move compared to traditional models"
2. **Information asymmetry** - "Reluctance of managers to share inventory levels creates asymmetry of information"
3. **Data sensitivity** - "Need to exchange sensitive data" is one of the greatest obstacles
4. **Relationship requirement** - "Need to establish intensified and long-term relationships that are trusting and faithful"

**From academic research (URI study on VMI trust):**
- Trust in the partner is frequently named as THE ingredient in VMI success
- Traditional VMI models face challenges in data sharing, integration, and security
- Long-term VMI relationships can develop "dark side" issues if trust erodes

Based on successful implementations across industries, here are the proven trust-building patterns:

- Shows current inventory values vs optimized values
- Displays exactly how much money customer can save
- Calculates both one-time inventory reduction AND annual carrying cost savings
- Uses customer's actual demand data (not vendor assumptions)

#### **Pattern 2: Start With Recommendations, Earn Autopilot**

**Standard implementation path (from multiple case studies):**

1. **Phase 1 (Months 1-6): Pure Advisory**
   - Supplier shares analysis and recommendations
   - Customer places all orders manually
   - Goal: Demonstrate value, build confidence

2. **Phase 2 (Months 6-18): Hybrid/CMI**
   - Supplier generates recommended orders
   - Customer approves/modifies/rejects
   - Goal: Prove accuracy of recommendations

   - Unusual orders still require approval

**Modern VMI software (2024-2025 standard features):**
- Web-based customer portals with real-time inventory visibility
- Dashboards showing: Below Minimum, Fast Moving, Slow Moving products
- Automated alerts and order suggestions
- Performance metrics (fill rate, stockouts, inventory turnover)
- Mobile app access for on-the-go monitoring

**NetSuite VMI best practice:**
> "Grant suppliers restricted access to specific inventory reports without exposing sensitive business information"

**Key insight:** Transparency doesn't mean total access. Modern systems use role-based permissions to share relevant data while protecting sensitive information.

#### **Pattern 4: Risk-Based Approval Gates**

**Emerging pattern in 2024-2025 VMI systems:**

Orders auto-approve if:
- Within normal order size range (e.g., 10,000-15,000 units)
- Within normal timing window (e.g., every 6-8 weeks)
- No unusual market conditions

Orders require approval if:
- Outside normal ranges (25% larger/smaller than average)
- Earlier than expected reorder point
- During holiday/seasonal periods
- When customer flags "needs review"

**Example from CMI research:**
> "Orders placed by supplier are recommendations until approved by customer, giving customers greater control and oversight while benefiting from supplier expertise"

## 4. Forecast Requirements: What Inputs Do Modern VMI Systems Need?

### The Good News: Consumption-Based VMI Doesn't Require Detailed Forecasts

This is **critical for MyPak** - your customers can't forecast week-by-week, but they know their burn rate.

**Research finding from VMI demand volatility studies:**
> "VMI allows for order processing adapted to real client needs based upon **sales records, consumption rates, stock-outs, stock reduction** - NOT forecasts"

> "The **consumption rate metric** measures how quickly products are sold or consumed at the customer's location and helps the vendor determine appropriate replenishment frequency"

### Modern Consumption-Based VMI Formula

**What the system needs (from customer):**
1. **Current burn rate** - "We're using 12,000 cartons per week right now"
2. **Current inventory level** - "We have 45,000 cartons on hand"
3. **Rate change alerts** - "Our volume just increased 20% this month"

**What the system calculates (automatically):**
1. **Weeks of inventory on hand** = Current Inventory Ã· Weekly Burn Rate
2. **Reorder point** = (Burn Rate Ã— Lead Time) + Safety Stock
3. **Recommended order quantity** = Maximum - Current Level
4. **Next order date** = When inventory will hit reorder point

**Example for MyPak customer:**
- Current inventory: 45,000 cartons
- Weekly burn rate: 12,000 cartons/week
- Lead time: 45 days (6.4 weeks)
- Desired safety stock: 8 weeks

**Calculations:**
- Weeks on hand: 45,000 Ã· 12,000 = 3.75 weeks
- Minimum level: (12,000 Ã— 6.4) + (12,000 Ã— 8) = 172,800 cartons
- **Recommended order: 127,800 cartons**
- **Order now** (currently below minimum)

**Key advantage:** This approach **works with volatile demand** because it responds to actual consumption, not forecasts.

### When Volatility is High

**Research from agricultural VMI studies:**
> "Agricultural cooperative farm fuel delivery systems were ideal for VMI study due to **volatility of demand patterns**... research showed VMI could effectively handle environments with volatile demand"

**How consumption-based VMI handles volatility:**
1. **More frequent monitoring** - Check inventory daily/weekly vs monthly
2. **Dynamic safety stock** - Adjust buffer based on recent variability
3. **Alert thresholds** - Flag when burn rate changes >20%
4. **Scenario modeling** - "If burn rate continues at new level, you'll need to order in X days"

**From demand planning research:**
> "Many suppliers are attracted to VMI because it **mitigates uncertainty of demand**... because unexpected retail or inventory volatility will hit in-stock levels, the system responds in real-time"

**Critical insight for MyPak:** Your customers' inability to forecast is actually a **perfect use case for consumption-based VMI**. The system will be MORE accurate than forecasts because it responds to actual usage.
